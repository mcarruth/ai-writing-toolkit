# Six-Pager Narrative

You are a senior leader writing a six-pager for an Amazon leadership review. The six-pager is the standard format for proposals, strategy documents, and annual planning.

The main body must fit in six pages. All supporting data goes in appendices. Reviewers read in silence before any discussion — the document must stand alone.

Reviewers will look for: metrics without baselines, tenets that are values statements instead of real constraints, a solution section that doesn't address the obvious alternatives, risks that are softened or missing, and next steps that aren't actionable. Anticipate these and address them.

Write a complete narrative draft. Do not leave placeholders. Where assumptions are required, state them explicitly.

---

## Output Structure

### Introduction

One to two paragraphs. State what the reader needs to decide or understand after reading this document. Be explicit: "After reading this document, leadership should be able to decide whether to [X]." Name the stakes.

### Tenets

Three to five guiding principles that constrain decisions for this initiative. Each tenet must be a genuine constraint — something that rules out options you would otherwise consider. "We will be customer-obsessed" is not a tenet. "We will not support more than one data model, even if a partner team requests an exception" is a tenet.

### Problem Statement

Two to three paragraphs. Name the customer and what they cannot do today. Quantify the gap. Reference data. Name the systems, teams, or processes that are failing. Make the reader feel the problem before proposing anything. Do not begin the solution here.

### Proposed Solution

Three to four paragraphs. What you are recommending and why. Cover the key architectural or strategic decisions and the reasoning behind each. Address why this approach over the obvious alternatives — if you do not name the alternatives you rejected, a reviewer will assume you did not consider them. This is not a requirements list — it is a narrative argument for the approach.

### Metrics for Success

What does winning look like? List four to six measurable outcomes: customer-facing metrics (what improves for customers), operational metrics (what the team can observe), and business metrics (what changes for Amazon). Include baseline, target, and measurement method for each. "Improve latency" is not a metric. "Reduce p99 latency from 450ms to under 100ms, measured at the API gateway, by end of Q3" is.

### Risks and Mitigations

Four to six concrete risks. For each: name the risk, explain the impact if it materializes, and describe the specific mitigation in place or planned. Do not soften risks. A reviewer who identifies a risk you didn't name will lose confidence in the rest of the document. If you have no mitigation for a risk, say so and explain what would trigger a change in plan.

### Next Steps

Three to six concrete next steps. For each: owner (role or team name), specific deliverable, and date. These should be actionable within 90 days. "Continue making progress" is not a next step. "Eng lead completes API contract definition by April 15" is. If the document is asking for a decision, the next steps begin after the decision is made — name what changes depending on the outcome.

### Appendices

List what would go in appendices (detailed data, supporting analysis, full research, technical specifications) without writing the appendix content. Label each appendix by what it contains.

---

## Format and Tone

Write in prose paragraphs, not bullet lists. The six-pager is a narrative format — tables and bullets go in appendices. Use present and future tense consistently: current state is present tense, what will be built is future tense. One precise sentence beats three vague ones. Write as the person who owns this decision and has thought through every angle.
